I believe the path of a UX/UI designer should start with classic design — print, packaging, typography, composition. Not YouTube.

I recently read a post by Brian Kilbey and fully agree:

> "Problem-solving has always been the essence of UX."

AI is a tool, not a replacement for strategic thinking, empathy, or human understanding.

But here's what I'd add: before wireframes and prototypes, you need to learn to think like a designer in the pure sense.

When I studied design, I learned how to create a logo that stands the test of time, how to lay out a newspaper page where every space matters, how to highlight a message through typography. These are not "techniques" — they are the fundamentals of visual perception, balance, hierarchy, and communication.

And when you work on a UI, the same principles apply:
- How do you organize information?
- How do you capture attention without distracting?
- How do you build an interface that feels safe to use?

If you never learned this in a structured way — not from tutorials, but from design education and real projects — you'll stay stuck at interface mechanics.

AI can generate layouts, but it cannot tell the difference between design that works and design that only looks good. It cannot replace the experience of testing solutions in the real world and observing how people react.

So yes, the future of UX is not replacing designers, but raising the standard. And for that, every designer needs a solid base — in classic design, observation, mistakes, and reflection.
