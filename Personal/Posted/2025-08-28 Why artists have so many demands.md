Why artists have so many "demands" — and why they're not about ego.

It's easy to see a rider — that long list of hotel, food, and setup requests — as entitlement. Another sign of the "diva" stereotype.

But after years of working with live visuals, projection mapping, and performance design, I've seen the truth: those lists aren't about luxury. They're about control in an environment built on chaos.

When you're on tour, everything changes daily.
New city. New room. New bed. New time zone. New crew.
Even the air feels different.

And when your job is to perform — whether with voice, movement, or presence — your body is your instrument. And like any instrument, it needs stability to function.

A bad night of sleep? That can ruin a vocal performance.
Unfamiliar food? It can trigger illness at the worst moment.
Inconsistent lighting or acoustics? They break focus before you even start.

So the rider isn't a list of wants. It's a tool for consistency.

A quiet room. A specific type of chair. The same tea at the same time.
Not because the artist is difficult — but because these small things create a repeatable routine. A sense of home in constant motion.

What looks like excess is often just preparation.
What looks like ego is often just discipline.

Touring isn't glamorous. It's exhausting.
And those conditions? They're not perks.
They're the minimum required to show up, night after night, and deliver something real.

I've designed live visuals for events where the smallest detail — light temperature, timing, spatial layout — changed the entire emotional impact. So I know: precision isn't obsession. It's respect for the work.
