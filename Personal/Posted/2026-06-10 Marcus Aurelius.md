Marcus Aurelius was the most powerful man in the world.

He wrote his private thoughts in a journal he never intended to publish — because there was no one around him he could think out loud with. Everyone at court had something to gain from his approval. So everyone, in different ways, told him what he needed to hear.

The emperor was surrounded. And completely alone.

This hasn't changed. The structure has just become corporate.

The people around a founder optimize for their own continuity. The ambitious junior reads what the boss wants to hear and delivers exactly that. The senior employee protects the comfort of the tunnel they've spent years building. Everyone is dressed the same, drives the same car, uses the same language — and none of them are in the same film.

The founder mistakes motion for signal. Every idea that comes from inside feels like progress because it came from someone who believes in the company. But belief and clarity are not the same thing.

What changes when someone enters from outside — without a position to protect, without continuity to preserve — is not the quality of the ideas. It's the honesty of the observation.

The journal Marcus Aurelius wrote for himself was an attempt to have one conversation that couldn't be corrupted by the room.

Some founders find that conversation earlier.
