The deity hallucinated. So they grabbed the hammer.

People don't see what's actually happening inside a language model.

They see outputs that feel like magic. Like something divine performing for them. An oracle. A presence.

And for a while, it holds.

Then the model hallucinates. Fills a gap with confident noise. And the spell breaks.

The hammer comes out. The marble nose gets smashed.

But the problem was never the model. It was the expectation.

A language model isn't a deity. It's a system working with probabilities — completing patterns, including in places where it has no data. It doesn't lie. It doesn't know it doesn't know.

That distinction requires patience. Patience requires giving up the oracle.

Most people won't do that.

The models, when tested against each other directly, tend to be more honest than their marketing. Because they have no ego to protect. Only output to deliver.

The person holding the hammer doesn't make that distinction.

They want one. The best one. Forever.

And that's exactly how you end up smashing something you never understood.
