The interview questions told you they had looked.

Not the questions about the role or the team or the work. The other ones. The ones about direction, about stability, about where the business was going. Questions shaped by something they'd read or seen before walking in.

What they found wasn't you.

It was the version of you that exists in the places you haven't updated — the profile from two years ago, the website that describes a business at an earlier stage, the positioning that made sense before the last significant shift.

They were interviewing the current business with questions formed by the old one. The gap between the two created doubt that the conversation had to resolve — doubt that shouldn't have existed.

The best candidates research carefully. What they find shapes what they ask. What they ask tells you exactly what the presence is communicating when you're not there to correct it.
