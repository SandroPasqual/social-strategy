They chose someone who charged less.

You found out later. Through a mutual contact, through a casual mention, through the kind of information that arrives after the decision is already made. You knew the competitor. You knew the work. There was no comparison.

But the prospect didn't know that.

They made the decision with what they had — two presences, side by side, signaling two different levels of seriousness. One price higher. One presence that didn't justify the gap.

The work would have justified it. The engagement would have proven it. But the decision was made before the work started — in the forty seconds they spent comparing what they could see.

You didn't lose on quality. You didn't lose on thinking. You didn't lose on value.

You lost before the conversation became a comparison. The presence placed you in a category where price became the deciding factor.

That's not a pricing problem.
