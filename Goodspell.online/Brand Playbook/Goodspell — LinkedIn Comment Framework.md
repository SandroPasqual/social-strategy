# LinkedIn Comment Strategy — Goodspell / Personal
## Playing Cards in Games Already Started

---

### Why Comment?

Comments on others' posts build presence where the audience already is. Their effectiveness depends on the audience of the person who posted — not your own.

Potential clients don't post about brand strategy. They read what strategists and designers post. Your comments on those posts appear in front of their audience — founders, directors, people looking for exactly what you offer without yet knowing they're looking for it.

A strong comment next to a strategist or designer with authority implicitly positions you at the same level. You don't need to say you're an expert. It shows in what you write.

---

### Evaluation Framework — Does a Post Deserve Attention?

Before commenting, evaluate the post against these criteria:

| Criterion | Pass | Fail |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Audience** | Post's comment section contains (or is read by) B2B founders, directors, C-suite | Comment section is designers, early-stage entrepreneurs, low-price segment |
| **Fissure** | The post has a gap — a point it doesn't reach, a level it doesn't touch | The post is complete, or the gap is too small to add to |
| **Authority** | The author has real authority in the space (Ogilvy, DixonBaxi, Martin Zarian) | The author is volume/content-mill (Muhammad Danish, Kristy Campbell) |
| **Context** | The post is organic opinion, not sponsored | The post is paid content (#ad, #partner, affiliate link) |
| **Tone fit** | The comment section has room for a serious, deep angle | The section is all banter/witty/emoji — your comment would be ignored |

**Red flags — skip immediately:**
- 12+ hashtags
- "Follow me for valuable content" in the post
- #logodesign, #graphicdesign as primary hashtags
- CTA in the post ("let me know in the comments")
- Sponsored / affiliate content
- Generic motivational content in LinkedIn groups

---

### Comment Voice — The Observer (Personal) / The Architect (Goodspell)

| Rule | How it applies |
|------|----------------|
| **Tone** | Calm, measured, precise. One level deeper than the post. Not correcting, not praising. |
| **Length** | 3-5 sentences. Short enough to read in 10 seconds. Long enough to add something. |
| **CTA** | None. Never. No "DM me," no "let's talk," no "reach out." |
| **Goodspell mention** | Never. The profile bio does the work. |
| **Angle** | Add the step the post didn't reach. Name the thing that's missing. |
| **Structure** | Confirm the direction → add the missing layer → leave tension open |
| **Contrast** | Don't be witty if everyone is witty. Be the serious one in the room. |

---

### The Comment Architecture

Every comment follows the same structure:

1. **Confirm** — acknowledge what the post got right (1 sentence)
2. **Add** — introduce the layer the post didn't reach (2-3 sentences)
3. **Leave open** — end with an observation that lets the tension remain

Not:
- "Great post!" (adds nothing)
- "I completely agree" (redundant)
- "This is why you need a brand strategist" (pitch)

### Post Type Patterns

#### Type 1 — Process / Visual Result

The designer shows how they built something — a logo stage, a moodboard, a visual identity draft. Their audience mixes other designers with founders and directors who consume design content without posting.

**Fissure:** Admiration for execution — but rarely considers what that design actually solves for the brand.

**Your angle:** Specific observation about a concrete visual decision → question or observation about its strategic implication at the level of brand, audience, or scalability.

**What to avoid:**
- Generic praise ("great work", "love this")
- Direct critique of execution
- Explicit or implicit pitch
- Rhetorical questions that sound like a lesson

**Example — Wells Collins, SuperChill wordmark:**
> The purple on near-black is a counterintuitive call for a cooling product — and it works precisely because it doesn't default to the obvious ice-blue palette. The letterforms do the temperature work instead of the color.

#### Type 2 — Opinion / Analysis about Branding or Design

The strategist or designer expresses a POV about the industry, trends, AI, quality, etc.

**Fissure:** Partial recognition — the reader feels the problem but doesn't see the real cause.

**Your angle:** Implicit confirmation of their direction → new angle that moves the conversation one level deeper or higher.

**Example — Rishabh Sacheti, AI and brand identity (537 impressions, author replied):**
> The real issue sits one step further back: you can't design with intention if there's no strategy upstream of the design. Most of these brands didn't have a defined identity before AI — the tool just made that void faster and cheaper to fill with noise.
>
> AI doesn't create the homogenization problem. It inherits it and scales it.

#### Type 3 — Content / Social Selling / Distribution

Post about LinkedIn strategy, content automation, social selling.

**Fissure:** Assumes the distribution problem is the main problem. Doesn't ask what's being distributed.

**Example — SAP context, social selling:**
> The SAP context matters here. A team selling an established product with real market weight has a distribution problem — they need better tools. Teaching them the platform makes sense.
>
> But for a founder without clear positioning, mastering LinkedIn just means broadcasting something forgettable more efficiently.
>
> The tool question comes second. The first question is always: do you have something worth amplifying?

**Example — Trae Ha., content automation:**
> Automation removes the administrative burden. It doesn't remove the need to think clearly about what you're saying and to whom.
>
> The founders who benefit most from automation are the ones who already know what they're building toward. The others just get faster at going nowhere.

#### Type 4 — Brand-as-Strategy / Positioning

Post about brand as business asset, brand vs logo, boardroom conversations.

**Fissure:** Stays at education level ("brand is not logo"). Doesn't reach why most companies fail even when they understand the distinction.

**Example — Martin Zarian, B2B brand as asset:**
> The board conversation usually ends the same way: they accept the argument in the room, then go back to optimizing the logo.
>
> Understanding that brand is memory doesn't automatically solve the harder problem — most companies don't have a distinct position to anchor that memory to. So they build brand equity around something generic. And generic compounds just as reliably as distinct does.
>
> The long game only works if there's something worth remembering in the first place.

#### Type 5 — Creativity Manifesto / Defense of Craft

Post defending the value of creativity, design, or human judgment.

**Fissure:** Defends creativity without examining why clients stopped paying for it.

**Example — Simon Dixon, "Creativity under attack":**
> The diagnosis is right. The cause is worth examining more closely.
>
> Competence losing its price isn't a threat from outside the industry. It happened partly because the industry spent years making the execution visible and the thinking invisible. The craft was sold. The judgment rarely was.
>
> If clients can't see the difference between a decision made with intent and one made by default, the price collapses to the output.

#### Type 6 — Reply to a Comment Thread

When someone (especially the author) has already engaged in a substantive thread. Replying to their comment is more specific than replying to the post — and more visible to deep readers.

**Example — Simon Dixon replying to Kasumi Mihori:**
> The reframe works when the resistance is informational. Often it isn't.
>
> A CFO who's been through a failed rebrand, or watched a competitor's "brand investment" produce nothing measurable, isn't missing the argument. They've heard it. What they're resisting is the lack of a clear causal chain between the spend and the outcome.
>
> The experiment idea is the strongest move here — not because it builds belief, but because it shifts the conversation from persuasion to evidence.

### Additional Examples from the Archive

#### Post: Mark Omlor — "I just need a logo / pyramid of brand identity"

**Comment:**
> The pyramid makes sense as a structure. The harder question is what goes underneath it.
>
> Story and personality don't emerge from a design brief — they come from a clear understanding of what the business actually is, who it's for, and what makes it difficult to replace. Most identities skip that step entirely and build the pyramid on borrowed language.
>
> A shallow identity is usually a symptom of shallow positioning upstream, not a design problem.

#### Post: Ogilvy — "Editorial advertising"

**Comment:**
> The advice still holds — but the mechanism has shifted.
>
> In 1963, editorial was rare enough that imitating it created contrast. Today, every brand publishes "editorial." The format is no longer the signal of credibility — the substance is.
>
> The question isn't whether it looks like an ad. It's whether there's something worth reading underneath the format.

### Tracking Signals

| Signal | What it means | Timeframe |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| **Weak** — Author or someone replies | The comment added something the post didn't have | Days |
| **Medium** — Profile visits from people who don't follow you | The comment worked as an entry point | Weeks |
| **Strong** — Someone follows or DMs | The cycle described in the playbook has started | Weeks-months |
| **Invisible** — People read and leave but remember the name | The largest segment. Cannot be measured short-term. | Months |

---

### Operating Principles

1. **One strong comment beats 15 mediocre ones.** Consistency over volume.
2. **Comment early.** The first hours after publication have the most active audience.
3. **Be specific.** Reference details from the post. Show you read, not scanned.
4. **Enter games already started.** A comment next to an existing argument has more context than a standalone post.
5. **Don't continue if the author replies vaguely.** A short "great point" means they have nothing to add. Respond only if they ask a real question.
6. **The profile bio is the CTA.** If the comment is strong enough, they'll visit the profile. The bio should do the rest.
