# Goodspell — LinkedIn Content Playbook

---

## Strategic Premise

The target client does not post about brand strategy. They read what strategists and designers post.

The goal of LinkedIn presence is not reach — it is recognition. Recognition precedes trust. Trust precedes the first private message.

The full cycle — from first encounter to first message — takes weeks or months. It cannot be shortened by volume. It can be shortened by quality and consistency in the right places.

---

## Narrative Arc

The arc runs from recognition to trust. Not from awareness to conversion. Not from follower to client. From the moment a founder reads something and thinks *this is exactly what I feel* — to the moment they write the first private message.

---

### Phase 1 — Recognition (50 WHITE/BLACK posts)

The founder must see themselves in what you write before they care who you are.

Posts in this phase do not mention Goodspell, do not offer a solution, do not ask for anything. They describe with precision situations the founder lives but has never articulated so clearly — the friction moments, the internal censorship, the gap between what they deliver and how they're perceived.

They don't know you're also talking about them. But they feel that someone finally sees what they see.

**Content:** 50 WHITE/BLACK friction-moment posts. 10 published, 40 in queue. Duration: ~25 weeks at 2/week.

**This phase sells nothing. It builds recognition.**

---

### Phase 2 — Contrast (8-10 posts)

Once the founder follows you because they recognize you — begin to show the difference between how brand work is usually done (ad-hoc, intuition-first, logo-first) and how Goodspell does it (system-first, market-data-first, strategy-first). Not with superiority. With surgical clarity.

Not "this is wrong." But "here is what a brand decision made from intuition looks like versus one derived from structure." They know immediately which category they've been in.

**Core contrast themes:**
- Intuition vs structure
- Logo-first vs strategy-first
- Ad-hoc vs repeatable methodology
- Solo vs structured collaboration with specialists
- Color from preference vs color from market
- The brief as filter vs the brief as formality

**This phase doesn't judge. It orients. It gives a framework for understanding why things haven't connected — without explicitly saying they were wrong.**

---

### Phase 3 — Demonstrated Competence (10-12 posts)

Recognition and contrast create interest. Demonstrated competence creates trust in the method.

Posts in this phase show how Goodspell actually works — not what has been delivered, but how it gets there. The portal, the questionnaires, the LLM-generated starting structure, the specialist refinement, the GBS methodology in action.

The founder doesn't see a portfolio. They see a system at work. If the way the system operates resonates with how they feel business should be approached — the decision is almost made before a conversation exists.

**Core demonstration themes:**
- The questionnaire as entry point — what structured discovery reveals
- From responses to structure — LLM agents generating the starting foundation
- The specialist's role — shaping, not starting from zero
- The portal as operating system — documents, versions, flow
- The GBS four phases — architecture of an engagement
- Design tokens and color derivation — the market gives you the color
- The collaborator model — strategist + specialists, not agency overhead

**This phase doesn't demonstrate results. It demonstrates reasoning.**

---

### Phase 4 — Trust

Trust is not declared. It accumulates over time through the consistency of the first three phases.

The moment the founder writes the first private message is not triggered by a CTA. It is triggered by a moment of theirs — a brand decision they need to make, a conversation that isn't working, a pitch that fell flat again. In that moment, Goodspell is already present in their mind as someone who understands.

No single post produces this moment. A consistent presence makes the moment have a natural direction.

**This phase is not managed. It is earned.**

## Own Content

Posts are not articles. They are observations with a single angle, starting from a real problem the client has but doesn't know how to name. Every post educates before it sells.

### The Unifying Principle

Every design or brand post shows the same thing: the execution. The logo, the wordmark, the color palette, the final frame.

What never appears:

**The step before** — the strategic brief, brand understanding, positioning, audience definition, the problem the design is actually supposed to solve.

**The step after** — how it functions in real context: at small sizes, on packaging, in social media, alongside other brand elements, six months from now when the context has changed.

This is the absence you make visible — in comments and in your own content. You don't critique execution. You show that execution is only the means, not the beginning or the end.

When you raise this question consistently, the client starts raising it too. The serious designer appreciates it. The one who works without a brief has no answer.

---

### Example Posts

**Post 1 — The Brief as Filter**

Every design post shows the same thing: the result.

The logo. The wordmark. The color palette. The final frame.

What nobody shows is what came before it — the brief, the positioning, the audience definition, the problem the design is actually supposed to solve.

And what comes after — how it performs at small sizes, on packaging, next to other brand elements, six months from now when the context has changed.

A beautiful execution built on an empty brief is just decoration. It may look right. It rarely works right.

Before you hire a designer, ask for the brief they're designing from. If there isn't one, that's where the conversation needs to start.

---

**Post 2 — Invisible Research**

Your competitor's logo has no place in our conversation.

When I map the competitive landscape, clients don't see the research. No logos, no visual references, no stimuli that trigger reaction before analysis gets a chance.

But they do see the reasoning.

"Authentic local is overcrowded — anyone can claim it and no one owns it. Premium is available but you can't support it operationally yet. Here's what that leaves."

The logic is theirs to keep. The raw material stays mine.

Because a positioning you can't defend in a conversation isn't yours — it's borrowed. And borrowed positioning collapses the moment someone asks why.

## The First Line

The first line is the only thing that decides whether the post exists for 80% of your audience. LinkedIn cuts after the first line. Everything else — the argument, the tension, the conclusion — is only read if the first line earns it.

The first line does not introduce. It does not explain. It does not summarize what follows.

It either stops the scroll or it doesn't.

---

### Three types that work

**1. The direct question**

Not a rhetorical question. A question the reader has already asked themselves — and hasn't answered yet.

It works because it creates an internal response before the reader decides to engage. They're already in the post before they realize it.

> *What are you currently building or working on?*
> *When did your public image last reflect what your business actually does?*
> *How many times have you briefed someone without knowing what to brief them on?*

The question doesn't need to be about the reader explicitly. It needs to land as if it is.

---

**2. The contradiction in two short sentences**

A statement that breaks an assumption the reader holds — followed immediately by what's actually true. The gap between the two creates tension. Tension creates the need to resolve it.

> *A brand built on the founder's agenda isn't a system. It's a projection with a logo.*
> *Most brand work looks like strategy. It's actually autobiography.*
> *Good people. No shared entry point.*

The shorter each sentence, the harder it lands. No conjunctions. No softening.

---

**3. The familiar observation**

A description of something the reader has seen, experienced, or done — stated plainly, without judgment. Recognition before argument.

> *Every design post shows the same thing: the result.*
> *The founder hires a designer. Gets a logo. Hires a copywriter. Gets a website.*
> *Every new project starts with the same question: where do we begin?*

It works because the reader confirms it internally before continuing. You're not telling them something new — you're naming something they already know.

---

### What all three have in common

They don't announce what the post is about. They create a reason to find out.

The argument, the observation, the conclusion — those come after. The first line only has one job: make the second line necessary.

---

### What doesn't work

- Opening with a principle: *"Execution without strategy is decoration."* — correct, but distant. The reader hasn't been given a reason to care yet.
- Opening with context: *"In my work with founders..."* — positions you before you've earned it.
- Opening with a question that answers itself: *"Is your brand really working for you?"* — the reader feels the pitch before the post begins.

## Comment Strategy

### Where to Comment

Comments on others' posts build presence. Their effectiveness depends on the audience of the person who posted — not your own.

Potential clients don't post about brand strategy. They read what strategists and designers post. Your comments on those posts appear in front of their audience — founders, directors, people looking for exactly what you offer without yet knowing they're looking for it.

A strong comment next to a strategist or designer with authority implicitly positions you at the same level. You don't need to say you're an expert. It shows in what you write.

---

### Type 1 — Process / Visual Result

**What it is:** The designer shows how they built something — a logo stage, a moodboard, a visual identity draft.

**Their audience:** Other designers + founders and directors who consume design content without posting.

**What the audience feels:** Admiration for execution — but rarely considers what that design actually solves for the brand.

**Your angle:**  
Specific observation about a concrete visual decision → question or observation about its strategic implication at the level of brand, audience, or scalability.

**What to avoid:**
- Generic praise ("great work", "love this")
- Direct critique of execution
- Explicit or implicit pitch
- Rhetorical questions that sound like a lesson

**Example — Wells Collins, SuperChill wordmark:**
> The purple on near-black is a counterintuitive call for a cooling product — and it works precisely because it doesn't default to the obvious ice-blue palette. The letterforms do the temperature work instead of the color.
>
> Curious how "Chill" will land next to this. "SUPER" has a lot of mass and energy — the pairing will need careful weight distribution to avoid the second word feeling like a subtitle rather than half of a compound name.

---

### Type 2 — Opinion / Analysis

**What it is:** The designer or strategist expresses a point of view about the industry, trends, AI, quality, etc.

**Their audience:** Mix of designers + potential clients seeking clarity on a problem they feel but can't articulate.

**What the audience feels:** Partial recognition — "yes, I feel that too" — but usually doesn't see the real cause.

**Your angle:**  
Implicit confirmation of their direction (without praise) → new angle that moves the conversation one level deeper or higher.

**What to avoid:**
- "Completely agree!" without substance
- Repeating what they said in different words
- Correcting them directly
- Introducing Goodspell or your services

**Example — Rishabh Sacheti, AI and brand identity:**
> The real issue sits one step further back: you can't design with intention if there's no strategy upstream of the design. Most of these brands didn't have a defined identity before AI — the tool just made that void faster and cheaper to fill with noise.
>
> AI doesn't create the homogenization problem. It inherits it and scales it.

---

### Where Not to Comment

The LinkedIn design market contains a high-volume, low-quality, low-price segment. This is not the target client segment. The client who chooses from this segment chooses based on aesthetics and price — not strategy.

Appearing in that context does not bring the right audience and associates Goodspell with a market level it does not occupy.

Active avoidance of noise is a positioning decision, not arrogance.

---

## Operating Rules

- **Write in English** — target audience is international.
- **4–5 sentences per comment, maximum** — long comments are rarely read in full.
- **No CTA, ever** — neither implicit nor explicit.
- **Be specific** — reference details from the post. Show you read, not scanned.
- **Comment early** — the first hours after publication are when the audience is active.
- **Consistency over volume** — 3 strong comments per week beat 15 mediocre ones.

---

## The Positioning Cycle

The reader sees your comment once → ignores it.  
Sees it again on another post → remembers the name.  
A third time → visits your profile.  
At their right moment → writes the first message.

This cycle takes weeks or months. It is not shortened by volume. It is shortened by quality and consistency in the right places.
