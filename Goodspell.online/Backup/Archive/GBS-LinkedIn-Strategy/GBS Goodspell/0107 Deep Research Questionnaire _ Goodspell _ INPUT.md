## 010707 Brand & Vision
### 01070701 Brand foundations — non-negotiable beliefs
These are the five convictions that define how Goodspell works. A client who contradicts any of these is not the right client for this engagement.
Clarity before style. Design must solve a business problem. If a client wants "something beautiful" without structure or meaning, the project has no purpose. Aesthetic satisfaction that does not serve a functional objective is not a deliverable Goodspell will produce.
Strategy before execution. A logo or identity without a strategic foundation is decoration. Without a documented conversation about positioning, audience, and purpose, the visual work is guesswork. No visual decision is made at Goodspell before the strategic foundation is confirmed.
Long-term consistency. A brand is not a file that gets delivered. It is a system that must be used correctly over time. The engagement does not end at delivery — it ends when the client can operate the system independently.
Real originality. Copying, imitating, or "make it like brand X" is not acceptable. Every brand system Goodspell builds is derived from the specific strategic reality of the client's business — not from visual references or category conventions.
Respect for process. Good results require active client participation. A client who wants fast results without engagement produces weak work. The GBS process is designed to be efficient with the client's time — but it is not designed to work without them.

## 010710 Audience Insights
### 01071001 Time Lost
The primary time cost for the Goodspell client is not in a single identifiable task — it is distributed across a chronic pattern of repeated explanations. Every new touchpoint, every new team member, every new designer brief requires a re-description of what the brand is and what it stands for. At an operational level, this accumulates across a hundred small decisions over a year.
Estimate: 3–6 hours per week in a business without a governing brand framework, across briefs, presentations, proposals, and internal communications. Over a year: 150–300 hours.
### 01071002 Revenue Impact
For the Established Operator audience: the revenue impact of the Maturity Gap is expressed as pricing pressure that should not exist. A firm operating at a $25,000–$100,000 engagement level but presenting with a brand that signals a $5,000–$15,000 studio is leaving a measurable delta on every deal.
For the Professional in Transition: the revenue impact is front-loaded. A practice that launches without a brand system calibrated to its positioning will spend the first 18–24 months correcting course — during which pricing is suppressed and client acquisition is slower than it should be.
### 01071003 Operational Inefficiency
Without a documented brand system:
Designer briefs are written from scratch for each project
Proposals require bespoke positioning language for each client
Presentations are rebuilt rather than adapted
New team members are briefed verbally, with accumulated imprecision over time
LinkedIn and website content is written without a governing voice framework, producing gradual drift
### 01071004 Specific Client Example
A founder in professional services had been operating for eight years with an original brand built in year one. When they entered a new market segment requiring enterprise-level clients, the brand — functional at its original scale — produced visible friction in the sales process. Prospects at the new level evaluated the firm against competitors whose external presentation matched their operational maturity. The pricing conversations were harder than they should have been. The founder was spending the opening 20 minutes of every sales call re-establishing credibility that the brand should have established before the meeting.
The intervention: a documented strategic repositioning, a revised positioning statement, and a visual system calibrated to the new audience. The first enterprise engagement closed without a pricing conversation.

## 010711 Communication & Design
### 01071101 Tone of Voice
Disciplined, direct, confident. The brand communicates as a firm that has made its decisions and is not seeking validation for them. It does not perform authority — it demonstrates it through structure, precision, and the quality of its thinking.
The register is that of a senior advisor in a peer conversation — not a vendor pitching, not a consultant presenting options. A peer who has seen this problem many times and has a documented framework for addressing it.
### 01071102 Brand sound and style
The brand commands the room through structure, not volume. Every sentence carries a specific claim. No aspirational language without evidence. No passive constructions. No questions as headlines.
The brand sounds like its documents look: organized, numbered, purposeful. The sentence structure is short and declarative at the headline level; precise and specific at the body level; direct and conclusive at the CTA level.
What it does not sound like: warm and approachable, enthusiastic, creative, inspirational, urgent, trendy. None of these registers belong in Goodspell's voice.
### 01071103 Visual Identity Reference Analysis
#### Reference 1 – McKinsey & Company
Not for the consulting category — for the structural signal. A homepage that contains almost no visual decoration. Authority communicated through text density, precise language, and the implicit confidence of a firm that does not need to explain itself. The lesson: restraint at the visual level, combined with precision at the language level, is the register of genuine authority.
#### Reference 2 — Pentagram
For the relationship between a firm's identity and its clients' identities. Pentagram's own visual presence is deliberately neutral — it recedes behind the work it does for others. The firm does not compete visually with the brands it builds. This is the model for Goodspell: one clear step behind every client brand it serves.
#### Reference 3 — Basecamp / 37signals
For the pricing and transparency model. Fixed prices, published publicly, with a clear rationale. No proposals. No negotiation. The pricing architecture is itself a positioning statement: we know what this is worth and we are confident enough to say so in public.
### 01071104 Inspirational visual brands
Dieter Rams / Braun The principle of "less, but better" applied to brand. Every element earns its place by serving a function. Nothing is added for visual interest. The authority comes from the precision of what is present, not from the abundance of what is shown.
Swiss International Style (grid-based typographic design) The visual language of structured systems: grid, hierarchy, precision. Not cold — disciplined. The kind of visual logic that signals that the firm behind it thinks in systems, not in decorative choices.
The Economist For the relationship between typography, color restraint, and authority. A publication that commands attention in a crowded newsstand not through visual complexity but through consistency, precision, and the confidence of a single, strong typographic voice.
