Framework Maturity Gap — defined
Week 1 · Post 2
The Maturity Gap — what it is and what it costs
The Maturity Gap is the distance between how good your business actually is — and how your brand makes it appear.It has three faces: The Visibility Gap — the work is excellent. Nobody can see it from the outside. The System Gap — there is no governing framework. Every new touchpoint is built from scratch. Every new contributor starts from zero. The Authority Gap — the brand cannot carry the pricing, the partnerships, or the conversations the business is ready for. Most businesses have at least one. Many have all three. The gap does not appear suddenly. It grows gradually — as the business matures and the brand does not. By the time it becomes visible, it has already been expensive for a while. Which of the three describes your situation most accurately?
Introduces the framework clearly. Three concrete faces make it diagnostic — the reader self-identifies. Question CTA generates comments without demanding action.
Principle Logic over Aesthetics
Week 2 · Post 1
Why a new logo does not solve a positioning problem
A logo does not fix a brand. It fixes a logo.When a business commissions a new visual identity without first documenting its positioning, it is spending significant resources to make the same unclear message look different. The result is a brand that looks more polished — but still requires the founder to explain it in every room it enters. The sequence matters: First — define what the business is, who it is for, and what makes it the right choice for that specific audience. Then — build the visual system to express that argument with precision. Most engagements reverse this order. Which is why most engagements need to be redone. A brand system is not a visual exercise. It is a business argument made visible. How many of your brand decisions were made before the positioning was documented?

Cycle 2 — Authority & Methodology
Framework System vs Deliverable
Week 3 · Post 1
The difference between a deliverable and a system
Most brand engagements produce deliverables. Very few produce systems.A deliverable is something you approve. A system is something you operate. A deliverable answers: does this look right? A system answers: why does this exist, and what governs the next decision? The difference becomes visible the first time a new designer, hire, or partner touches the brand after delivery. With a deliverable — they guess. They interpret. They call the founder. With a system — they open the document. They find the argument. They execute correctly. Without a call. This is not a question of quality. It is a question of architecture. A well-built system does not require Goodspell in the room. It does not require the founder in the room. It works because the reasoning is documented — not because someone remembers it. When was the last time your brand required you to explain it to someone who should have already understood?

Principle Fixed scope / Absolute Coherence
Week 4 · Post 1
Why fixed scope is a strategic decision, not a commercial one
Range-based pricing signals negotiability. Fixed pricing signals confidence in defined value.When a brand strategy engagement has a fixed scope and a fixed price, it communicates something before the conversation begins: The firm knows what the problem requires. It has done this before. It is not improvising the scope to fit the budget. Flexible scope has the opposite effect. It signals that the process is uncertain, the methodology is approximate, and the outcome is negotiable. For complex buyers — founders and operators who have been through at least one engagement that produced deliverables but not outcomes — this distinction is not subtle. They notice immediately. A fixed scope is not a constraint. It is a commitment — to a specific problem, a specific methodology, and a specific outcome. The buyer's question is not "how much does this cost." It is "can I trust that this will produce what it promises." Fixed scope answers that question before it is asked. What does variable pricing signal to a serious buyer in your category?
Reframes fixed pricing as a strategic signal, not a commercial limitation. Speaks directly to the sophisticated buyer profile.

Why I stopped doing design work
I did not stop because the design work was bad. I stopped because it was not the part that mattered most.For 25 years, I worked at the intersection of strategy and execution. I delivered visual systems, brand identities, and communication frameworks to companies across Europe and the US. The work was good. The clients were serious. The results were real. But the pattern that appeared repeatedly was not about design. It was about comprehension. Clients who came in asking for a rebrand almost always had a positioning problem they had not yet named. The visual work was downstream of a strategic decision that had never been made explicitly. So I started making that decision the engagement — not the deliverable. Goodspell Brand Strategy exists because the most important part of a brand engagement happens before any visual decision is made. What I do now is not a pivot away from the work. It is a pivot toward the part of the work that produces durable results. At what point did you realize the surface problem was not the real problem?

The cost of addressing it after — in lost deals, delayed hires, or an investor conversation that required repair — is harder to calculate and always higher. The gap does not get smaller on its own. Framework Strategic Authority
Week 6 · Post 1
What Strategic Authority actually means
Strategic Authority is not a feeling. It is a condition — and it can be built deliberately.A brand has Strategic Authority when: A prospect can evaluate it without speaking to the founder first. A new hire can understand what the business stands for from the documentation alone. A partner can represent the brand in a conversation without calling for guidance. A senior buyer can justify the engagement to their board — because the brand's logic is already visible. None of this happens through good design alone. It happens through a governed system — where every decision is traceable to a documented argument, and every contributor works from the same foundation. Strategic Authority is not the result of looking credible. It is the result of being coherent — consistently, across every context where the brand appears. The visual is the last mile. The system is the infrastructure. Does your brand currently give a senior buyer the evidence they need — before you walk into the room?
Defines the concept through concrete, testable conditions. Reframes the outcome as infrastructure, not aesthetics. CTA is a direct diagnostic question.
