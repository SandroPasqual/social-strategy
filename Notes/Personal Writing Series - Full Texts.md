## 10 texts. Sandro's voice. Distilled from notes (May 23, 2026).

### 1. If you smoke, smoke

If you smoke, smoke.

Not while scrolling, not while talking, not while half-thinking about something else. Actually smoke. Feel it. Be there for it.

I'm not saying it's good for you. I'm saying that if you're going to do it, do it with full attention. Because the moment you split your attention — phone in one hand, cigarette in the other — you've lost both. You didn't smoke. You performed smoking while your mind was somewhere else entirely.

We've gotten very good at this. Doing things without being there for them. The phone trained us well.

The strange thing is: if you actually smoked with full attention, you'd probably smoke less. Because you'd have to decide, consciously, every single time. No automatic reach. No reflex. A real choice.

That's the only habit the phone accidentally taught us to break — by making everything else so thin, presence itself became radical.

### 2. Fake nerds

There was a time when being a nerd meant something specific. You were inside the machine — literally. Taking it apart, putting it back together, pushing it past what it was designed to do. Servers, code, sites built from nothing. You were accused of wasting time on something no one understood yet.

Now the same people who accused you are on their phones six hours a day. Scrolling, posting, consuming. Screens all the way down.

The difference is they have a social layer to stand on. It's Instagram, not a terminal. It looks like connection, not obsession. So it doesn't count as the same thing.

We were nerds. They're just online.

At least no one's accusing us of anything anymore. We're all in the machine now. Most people just don't know which part they're in.

### 3. Off Grid For Three Stops

I got on the metro the other day and felt something strange.

Everyone was on their phone. I had mine in my pocket. And for a few minutes I just stood there, looking at faces — a jawline, a crooked nose, someone's expression caught between two thoughts.

It felt good. Quietly good.

Then I got bored of feeling good and took the phone out.

But before I did — I looked. Really looked. Old habit from drawing. You train yourself to catch the thing that makes a face that face, the detail that won't wait, because you never know when you'll need to put it on paper.

That reflex didn't leave. It just has fewer and fewer moments to run.

### 4. I remember boredom

I remember boredom.

Not the restless kind that demands to be fixed. The kind where you'd sit by the window for an hour, watching for the trolleybus, and that was enough. You were just there, with your thoughts, and your thoughts were company.

I didn't read much. When I did, it was heavily filtered — science fiction, other worlds, other logics. I had no patience for other people's inner lives on the page. Too close, too familiar, too often toxic. I preferred invented universes to borrowed ones.

Boredom back then was the gap where everything else grew. Music found you there. Drawing happened there. Ideas that needed silence to form — they lived in that gap.

I still know what to do with my own thoughts. That part didn't leave.

But the window is gone. The trolleybus wait is gone. The moment I feel the edge of that silence now, the phone appears. Not because I decided. Because it's faster than deciding.

An addict doesn't choose the dose. They just reach.

### 5. Saved by the bell

When the phone buzzes mid-conversation, something happens that we don't like to name honestly.

Relief.

Not curiosity about who it is. Relief that the complexity in front of you — the face, the argument, the unresolved thing hanging in the air — has been interrupted by something flat and manageable. One notification. One vector. No texture, no ambiguity, no demand to actually be present for another human being's reality.

We pick it up because it's easier than staying.

We call it a habit. It's closer to an exit.

### 6. We're all wet

We can't blame anyone for not finishing the film. For reading the summary instead of the book. For skipping to the chorus and calling it enough.

We do it too.

Not because we're lazy. Because the architecture of attention has changed for all of us — quietly, without asking permission. The film got longer and our patience got shorter and somewhere in between we started negotiating with everything that asks for our time.

Thirty minutes into something good and the hand reaches for the phone. Not because the film is bad. Because thirty minutes of uninterrupted presence has become genuinely difficult.

We are all downstream of the same current. Accusing each other of drowning while we're all wet.

### 7. The first stupid thought, dressed up as sacred

Something has happened to our ability to digest complexity. Not suddenly — slowly, then all at once.

After an event, we used to sit with it. Turn it over. Ask what actually happened, what it meant, what we might do differently. That processing took time and silence and a willingness to be wrong.

The phone offered something simpler: one vector. One feed, one angle, one tone. No need to correlate a thousand signals or read between lines or wonder what someone actually needs beneath what they're saying. The vector decides. You follow.

And once you've followed long enough, the vector becomes instinct. You stop questioning it because it feels like you. You call it intuition. Experience. Faith. The first thing you felt.

The first thing you felt is now the last word.

We don't go back to check. We don't revise. We defend the original read until we run out of breath — bombing the conversation with justifications, holding the line on something we formed in three seconds before we knew anything.

The first stupid thought, dressed up as sacred.

### 8. I, published

I write something and immediately the categories appear, uninvited.

Is this a blog post? A social post? A note? An essay?

The labels matter now because everything written assumes a destination. And every destination assumes an audience. And every audience assumes approval — likes, shares, the small dopamine signal that says: someone else thinks this too.

We tell ourselves we're being social. And we are. But underneath that is something older — the need to synchronize. To send a signal into the dark and hear it come back from another direction.

You're not alone in this.

That's what we're really posting for. Not to be seen. To be recognized.

The tragedy is that the platforms figured this out before we did. And they built the machine around it.

---

### 9. Marketable

Not everything worth writing needs to be marketable.

Some things exist because they need to exist — not because an audience is waiting for them. A thought fully formed, a moment captured, a question you sat with long enough to see it from all sides. That has value independent of distribution.

But we stopped treating it that way. Every text now has a job: to perform, to grow reach, to convert, to prove something. Even the act of writing itself has become a lead generation channel.

I'm not above it. I publish too. The need to be recognized is real and human.

But between "I need to write this" and "this is ready to be read" there's a gap we keep collapsing. And the collapse is costing us something — the writing that exists for its own sake, the thought that wasn't edited for approval, the sentence that didn't have to earn its keep.

The opposite of MVP isn't polish. It's the version that exists without a market.

---

### 10. While I Still Know

Someone close to me died recently.

And I noticed something I wasn't prepared for. The way we remember people — it's always the last version. The tired one. The one diminished by time or illness or distance. The young person, the sharp one, the one full of questions and bad decisions and energy — that one is gone. Even the people who knew them then can no longer fully access it.

Memory degrades. The early versions don't survive.

So I started writing. Not for publication. Not for approval. For the people who will know me only in the last chapter — so they have something from the middle of the book. So they know how I thought, what bothered me, what I found funny, what I couldn't let go of.

Not a legacy. Just evidence that I was here, like this, when I was.

Write while you still know who you are. You won't always.
