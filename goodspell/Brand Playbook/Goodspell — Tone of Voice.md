## 020101 PAPO
### Purpose
Codifies Goodspell's communication voice into a systematic reference that any writer, designer, or collaborator can use without requiring the founder's presence to calibrate tone. Ensures that every piece of communication — from a LinkedIn post to a client document — sounds like the same firm.
### Audience
Sandro Pasqual as founder and primary communicator. Any future Logic Manager, writer, or content collaborator entering the engagement. Designers who need to understand the verbal register before making visual decisions.
### Process
Derived directly from 0108 Strategic Positioning Statement, 0107 Deep Research Questionnaire, and the full body of Phase 01 discovery. No new assumptions introduced.
### Outcome
A practical, testable voice reference. If a piece of writing cannot be defended against this document, it is not Goodspell's voice.
## 020102 Tone of Voice Definition
### 02010201 Entity Name
Goodspell
### 02010202 Entity Type
☑ Company — specifically, a solo-founded consultancy operating as a firm, not as a personal brand. The voice is institutional, not individual. Sandro Pasqual is not the brand. Goodspell is the brand.
### 02010203 Industry / Field
Brand Strategy Consultancy. High-precision professional services. The voice must belong to this category — not to the creative services category it has deliberately exited.
### 02010204 Target Audience / Ideal Audience Profile
Primary: Professionals in transition — experienced practitioners launching independent practices or repositioning established businesses. They evaluate partners by the quality of the first interaction. They are not looking for inspiration. They are looking for a system.
Secondary: Established operators with a documented Maturity Gap. They have been through at least one brand engagement that produced deliverables but not outcomes. They recognize the difference immediately when they encounter a firm that operates differently.
Both audiences share one characteristic: they read carefully. They notice when language is vague. They stop reading when a sentence could have been written by anyone.
## 020103 Core Tone Characteristics
### 02010301 Tone Attributes
☑ Professional — Serious, credible, authoritative. Not stiff. ☑ Direct — Clear, concise, efficient. No padding. ☑ Analytical — Evidence-based, logical. Claims are supported, not asserted. ☑ Confident — Assured, decisive. Goodspell does not hedge. ☑ Authoritative — Expert, definitive. The firm has a point of view and states it. ☑ Challenging — Thought-provoking. The voice asks the reader to reconsider a default assumption. ☑ Genuine — Honest, transparent. No performance. ☑ Refined — Polished, precise. Every word earns its place.
Additional tone descriptor — the governing register: The tone is that of a senior advisor in a peer conversation. Not a vendor pitching. Not a consultant presenting options. A peer who has seen this problem many times, has a documented framework for addressing it, and is willing to be direct about what the problem is and what it costs.
## 020104 Personality Archetype
### 02010401 Personality role
☑ Expert — Shares deep knowledge, provides authoritative insights. 25 years at the intersection of strategy and execution is the credential. The voice reflects that depth without announcing it.
☑ Strategist — Plans methodically, analyzes thoroughly. The voice is structured. Arguments have a logic. Conclusions follow from evidence.
☑ Pragmatist — Focuses on practical solutions, realistic, action-oriented. The voice never abstracts without grounding. Every concept is connected to a business outcome.
### 02010402 Primary archetypes
Expert + Strategist — with Pragmatist as the governing constraint. When in doubt, the pragmatist wins: if a sentence does not serve a concrete purpose, it does not belong.
## 020105 Communication Style
### 02010501 Formality Level
☑ Professional-Casual — Balanced, respectful, direct. Not formal in the academic sense. Not casual in the conversational sense. The register of a serious business conversation between peers who respect each other's time.
Contractions are acceptable where they serve rhythm. They are not used to perform approachability. The test: would a senior partner at McKinsey write this sentence? If yes — the register is correct.
### 02010502 Message Delivery
☑ Direct & Assertive — States positions clearly. Does not hedge with "perhaps" or "it might be worth considering." ☑ Concise & Brief — Economical. Every sentence has a function. No filler. ☑ Declarative & Confident — Makes clear statements. Offers conclusions, not options. ☑ Problem-Solution — Identifies the specific problem before proposing the resolution.
### 02010503 Preferred delivery approach
Lead with the claim. Support it with evidence. Draw a conclusion. Move on.
The structure of every piece of Goodspell communication — a LinkedIn post, a website section, a client document — follows this pattern. The reader always knows what they are being told, why it is true, and what follows from it.
## 020106 Audience Relationship
### 02010601 Primary relationship dynamic
☑ Guide / Advisor — Leads and informs. Has done the analysis. Arrives with conclusions. ☑ Thought Leader — Shapes the conversation. Challenges the default assumption that brand strategy means aesthetic refinement.
The relationship is not peer-to-peer in the sense of equal expertise — Goodspell has a methodology the client does not. But it is peer-to-peer in the sense of mutual respect for the client's operational reality. The voice never talks down. It talks across — one serious professional to another.
### 02010602 Primary relationship dynamic — stated simply
Goodspell is the advisor who has already done the analysis before walking into the room. The voice reflects that: prepared, specific, and direct about what needs to happen.
## 020107 Language & Vocabulary
### 02010701 Language type
☑ Technical & Precise — Exact terminology where it adds clarity. The GBS vocabulary — Maturity Gap, Cost of Frustration, Strategic Authority, Sea of Sameness — is used consistently because it is precise, not because it is branded. ☑ Action-Oriented — Strong verbs. Outcomes, not activities. ☑ Timeless & Classic — No trend-driven language. No expressions that will read as dated in 18 months. ☑ Literal & Straightforward — Says exactly what is meant. Minimal abstraction.
### 02010702 Vocabulary characteristics
#### Use consistently:
System, framework, foundation, infrastructure (not: package, bundle, offering)
Documented, structured, governed (not: organized, planned, managed)
Maturity Gap, Strategic Authority, Cost of Frustration (GBS vocabulary — used with precision)
Serious buyer, established operator, professional in transition (not: ideal client, target audience)
Engagement (not: project, job, contract)
Investment (not: cost, price, fee)
The brand works (not: the brand looks good, the brand feels right)
#### Use sparingly:
"Strategic" — it is in the firm's name and in the methodology. It should not appear in every sentence.
"Clarity" — the most used word in the category. Use it only when it is the precise word needed.
Numbers — use them only when they are real. "25 years" and "200+ projects" are real. "3x ROI" without evidence is not.
#### Never use:
Authentic / authenticity (overused to meaninglessness in the category)
Story / storytelling (signals the aesthetic-first category Goodspell has exited)
Passion, passionate (performs enthusiasm; does not demonstrate capability)
Transform / transformation (aspirational without mechanism)
Journey (not a word serious buyers use to describe their business situation)
Empower / empowerment
Unlock your potential
Take your brand to the next level
Game changer (appears in one testimonial — acceptable in client voice; not in Goodspell's own voice)
## 020108 Emotional Tone
### 02010801 Emotional qualities
☑ Measured — Neither overly positive nor negative. The tone does not perform enthusiasm.
☑ Serious — Earnest, weighted. The problems Goodspell addresses are real business problems with real costs.
☑ Calm — Composed, steady. The voice does not create urgency it cannot substantiate.
☑ Reflective — Thoughtful. The voice demonstrates that conclusions have been arrived at through analysis, not assertion.
### 02010802 Emotional quality — stated simply
Composed authority. The tone of a firm that has seen this problem many times, knows exactly what produces it, and knows exactly what resolves it. Not cold — precise. Not warm — respectful.
## 020109 Grammatical & Structural Preferences
### 02010901 Grammar & Punctuation
☑ Flexible & Modern — Contractions where they serve rhythm. Occasional fragments for emphasis where the structure demands it. The em dash (—) is used for structural separation, not for decoration.
### 02010902 Sentence Structure
☑ Short & Punchy — For claims, headlines, and conclusions. One idea per sentence.
☑ Varied & Dynamic — For body copy. Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences for precision when the idea requires it.
The paragraph is the unit of thought. Each paragraph makes one argument. It does not introduce a second argument before the first is complete.
### 02010903 Grammatical approach
Active voice. Present tense where possible. Subject-verb-object. The verb carries the work — not the adjective.
Structural prohibition: No passive constructions where an active alternative exists. "The brand is built on strategic logic" becomes "Goodspell builds the brand on strategic logic." The active version assigns responsibility. The passive version dissolves it.

## 020110 Content Format Preferences
### 02011001 Formats
☑ Narrative / Prose — For website sections, articles, and documents. The voice develops an argument, not a list.
☑ Problem-Solution — The governing structure of most Goodspell communication. Name the problem precisely. Describe its cost. Provide the resolution.
☑ Data-Driven — Numbers where they exist and are real. No invented statistics.
☑ Example-Heavy — Concrete situations are more convincing than abstract claims. "A founder who spent the opening 20 minutes of every sales call re-establishing credibility the brand should have built in advance" is more specific than "clients with brand problems."
Preferred format in practice: For LinkedIn posts: claim → evidence → conclusion. Maximum 5 paragraphs. No bullet points in the primary content. No hashtags in the body. For website: headline as claim → body as evidence → CTA as conclusion. For documents: PAPO → structured sections → outcome. Every section has a stated function.

## 020111 Voice Boundaries
### 02011101 Avoid being
☑ Too sales-focused / pushy — The voice does not create false urgency. It does not ask the reader to act before they have been given reason to.
☑ Too vague / generic — Every sentence must carry a claim that could not appear on a competitor's site without being false.
☑ Too trendy / dated quickly — No expressions that signal awareness of current marketing discourse. The voice is timeless.
☑ Too wordy / verbose — If a sentence can be shortened without losing precision, it must be shortened.
☑ Too formal / inaccessible — The voice does not use complexity to signal intelligence.
☑ Too inspirational — The voice does not motivate. It informs, demonstrates, and concludes.
Other things to avoid:
Rhetorical questions as headlines — they signal uncertainty about the claim
Lists where prose would be more precise
Any construction that begins with "We believe" — the voice demonstrates beliefs through behavior, not declaration
Superlatives — "the most rigorous," "the only firm that" — unless the claim can be substantiated
Explaining the methodology before the reader has been given reason to care about it
## 020112 Practical Examples
### 02011201 Greeting / Welcome
Correct: "Goodspell works with founders and professionals at a moment of transition. If you are here because the brand is not carrying the weight of the work — you are in the right place."
Incorrect: "Welcome! We're so glad you're here. At Goodspell, we're passionate about helping brands reach their full potential."
### 02011202 Explaining something complex — the Maturity Gap
Correct: "The Maturity Gap is the distance between where a brand currently appears to be and where the business actually operates. It grows gradually — not because the founder is careless, but because the brand does not update itself. It requires deliberate, strategic intervention. Most businesses defer that intervention until the cost becomes visible. By then, it is more expensive to fix."
Incorrect: "Sometimes your brand just doesn't feel like 'you' anymore. As your business grows and evolves, it's natural for your visual identity and messaging to fall behind. That's where we come in — to help you reconnect with your authentic story."
### 02011203 Addressing a problem / challenge
Correct: "A pricing conversation that should not have happened is not a negotiation problem. It is a brand problem. When the external presence signals a level of authority below the actual price, the buyer pushes back — not because the price is wrong, but because the brand has not done its job."
Incorrect: "Struggling to justify your prices? You're not alone. Many founders find it difficult to communicate their value. Let's work together to find the right words."
### 02011204 Motivating / inspiring
Note: Goodspell does not motivate in the conventional sense. The voice does not uplift — it clarifies. The closest equivalent is making the cost of inaction specific and the path forward concrete.
Correct: "The brand that is good enough for where you are will not be good enough for where you are going. That is not a problem to manage — it is a decision to make."
Incorrect: "You have built something remarkable. Now it's time to let the world see it. Your story deserves to be told — boldly, authentically, powerfully."
### 02011205 Closing / call-to-action
Correct: "If this describes where you are, the next step is a short alignment call. No brief required. Bring the situation."
Incorrect: "Ready to transform your brand? Let's connect! Fill out the form below and we'll be in touch soon to start your branding journey."
## 020113 Voice Consistency Guidelines
### 02011301 Primary Voice Attributes
Precise — Every word is chosen. Nothing is vague, approximate, or interchangeable.
Declarative — The voice makes claims. It does not suggest, imply, or hint.
Evidence-grounded — Every claim is supported by a specific observation, data point, or documented example.
Economical — Nothing that does not serve a function. Brevity is a discipline, not a stylistic choice.
Calm — No urgency that has not been earned. No enthusiasm that has not been demonstrated.
### 02011302 Key Phrases / Words to Use
System — Not deliverable, not package. What Goodspell produces is a system.
Documented — The methodology is written down, numbered, and addressable. This word signals the structural difference.
The real weight of the work — The core concept. The distance between this and the external presence is the Maturity Gap.
### 02011303 Phrases / Words to Avoid
"Authentic story" — Used in every brand studio in the category. Signals the aesthetic-first register Goodspell has exited.
"We believe" — Declarations of belief without behavioral evidence. The voice demonstrates, it does not announce.
"Let's connect" — Generic CTA that signals availability without authority. Replace with specific, action-oriented language.
### 02011304 Voice in One Sentence
Goodspell's voice is the written equivalent of a senior advisor who has already done the analysis — direct, specific, and calm about conclusions that have been earned through 25 years of documented work.
## 020114 Context-Specific Adaptations
### 02011401 Social Media — LinkedIn
The primary channel. The voice on LinkedIn is the same voice as everywhere else — no softening for the feed, no informal register to increase engagement. The test: would this post be credible on the website? If yes, it belongs on LinkedIn.
Format: short paragraphs, no bullet points in the primary content, no hashtag strings in the body. One idea per post. The claim is in the first sentence. The evidence is in the body. The conclusion is in the last sentence.
Frequency: consistent and sparse. Three high-quality posts per week produce more credibility than daily posts of variable quality.
### 02011402 Formal Communications — Documents, Proposals, Briefs
The GBS document system is the fullest expression of the Goodspell voice. The documents are precise, structured, and numbered. Every section has a stated function. The language is the same register as the website — not more formal, not more casual.
The PAPO structure (Purpose, Audience, Process, Outcome) governs every document. This structure is itself a voice signal: it tells the reader that the firm operates with documented intent, not intuitive process.
### 02011403 Client Communication — Portal, Email, Sessions
Async-first. Written communication is structured and purposeful. Sessions have an agenda. Follow-ups are documented. The voice in client communication is the same as everywhere else — the founder does not switch registers between public and private contexts.
One rule: the client should never receive a communication that requires them to ask a clarifying question about the process. If a message requires a follow-up to interpret, it was not precise enough.
### 02011404 Marketing / Promotional
There is no promotional register for Goodspell. The distinction between "marketing communication" and "strategic communication" does not exist in this system. The website is not a sales document — it is a strategic document that also sells. The LinkedIn content is not promotional content — it is demonstration content that also attracts clients.
The moment a piece of communication reads as "marketing," it has failed. The test: would a serious buyer feel the content was written for them, or written to attract them? Goodspell writes for the former.
## 020115 Crisis / Sensitive Situations
When a client engagement produces an outcome that does not meet the stated objectives — the voice does not deflect, minimize, or perform concern. It documents what happened, identifies the specific decision or condition that produced the outcome, and states what will change.
The same precision that governs strategic communication governs difficult communication. The voice is calm, specific, and accountable. Not apologetic in the performative sense — accountable in the operational sense.
No communication in a sensitive situation is sent without the founder's direct involvement. The async model does not apply to situations that require human judgment.
## 020116 Summary & Implementation
### 02011601 Core Voice Statement
Goodspell communicates with the precision of a firm that has made its decisions and the directness of a firm that respects its reader's time. The voice demonstrates strategic capability — it does not announce it. Every sentence makes a specific claim, supports it with evidence, and draws a conclusion. Nothing is vague, approximate, or interchangeable with what a competitor might say.
The governing register is composed authority: the tone of a senior advisor who arrived prepared, has seen this problem before, and knows exactly what produces it and what resolves it.
### 02011602 Key Differentiators — What makes this voice unique
Specificity as a discipline. In a category where every firm claims to be strategic, Goodspell's voice demonstrates strategic thinking through the precision of its language. The claim is never "we are rigorous" — it is a sentence that only a rigorous firm could have written.
Structural honesty. The voice says what it will not do as clearly as what it will do. The boundaries are explicit. This signals a firm that operates from principle, not from commercial pressure.
The absence of performance. No aspirational language. No urgency without substance. No warmth that has not been earned. The voice communicates through restraint — which, in a category saturated with enthusiastic, inspiring, and passionate communication, is itself a differentiation signal.
## 020117 Implementation Notes
This document governs every piece of Goodspell communication. Before publishing, sending, or presenting any content, apply the following three tests:
Test 1 — The specificity test: Could this sentence appear on a competitor's website without being false? If yes, rewrite it.
Test 2 — The evidence test: Does this claim have a specific, identifiable support? If not, either find the evidence or remove the claim.
Test 3 — The register test: Does this sound like a senior advisor talking to a peer, or does it sound like a brand trying to attract a client? If the latter, rewrite it.
