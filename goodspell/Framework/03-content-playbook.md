# Goodspell — LinkedIn Content Playbook

---

## Strategic Premise

The target client does not post about brand strategy. They read what strategists and designers post.

The goal of LinkedIn presence is not reach — it is recognition. Recognition precedes trust. Trust precedes the first private message.

The full cycle — from first encounter to first message — takes weeks or months. It cannot be shortened by volume. It can be shortened by quality and consistency in the right places.

---

## Narrative Arc

The arc runs from recognition to trust. Not from awareness to conversion. Not from follower to client. From the moment a founder reads something and thinks *this is exactly what I feel* — to the moment they write the first private message.

---

### Phase 1 — Recognition

The founder must see themselves in what you write before they care who you are.

Posts in this phase do not mention Goodspell, do not offer a solution, do not ask for anything. They describe with precision situations the founder lives but has never articulated so clearly — the psychology behind brand decisions, the systems corrupted from within, the founder's own agenda infiltrated into strategy.

They don't know you're also talking about them. But they feel that someone finally sees what they see.

**This phase sells nothing. It builds recognition.**

---

### Phase 2 — Contrast

Once the founder follows you because they recognize you — begin to show the difference between how things are usually done and how they should be done. Not with superiority. With surgical clarity.

Not "classic consultants are wrong." But "here is what a brand decision made from fear looks like versus one made from clarity." They know immediately which category they've been in until now.

**This phase doesn't judge. It orients. It gives a framework for understanding why things haven't connected — without explicitly saying they were wrong.**

---

### Phase 3 — Demonstrated Competence

Recognition and contrast create interest. Demonstrated competence creates trust in the method.

Posts in this phase show how you think applied to concrete situations — not what you've delivered, but how you got there. The research process, the logic behind a positioning decision, the questions asked before any execution begins.

The founder doesn't see a portfolio. They see a mind at work. If the way you think resonates with how they feel business should be approached — the decision is almost made before a conversation exists.

**This phase doesn't demonstrate results. It demonstrates reasoning.**

---

### Phase 4 — Trust

Trust is not declared. It accumulates over time through the consistency of the first three phases.

The moment the founder writes the first private message is not triggered by a CTA. It is triggered by a moment of theirs — a brand decision they need to make, a conversation that isn't working, a pitch that fell flat again. In that moment, you are already present in their mind as someone who understands.

No single post produces this moment. A consistent presence makes the moment have a natural direction.

**This phase is not managed. It is earned.**

## Own Content

Posts are not articles. They are observations with a single angle, starting from a real problem the client has but doesn't know how to name. Every post educates before it sells.

### The Unifying Principle

Every design or brand post shows the same thing: the execution. The logo, the wordmark, the color palette, the final frame.

What never appears:

**The step before** — the strategic brief, brand understanding, positioning, audience definition, the problem the design is actually supposed to solve.

**The step after** — how it functions in real context: at small sizes, on packaging, in social media, alongside other brand elements, six months from now when the context has changed.

This is the absence you make visible — in comments and in your own content. You don't critique execution. You show that execution is only the means, not the beginning or the end.

When you raise this question consistently, the client starts raising it too. The serious designer appreciates it. The one who works without a brief has no answer.

---

### Example Posts

**Post 1 — The Brief as Filter**

Every design post shows the same thing: the result.

The logo. The wordmark. The color palette. The final frame.

What nobody shows is what came before it — the brief, the positioning, the audience definition, the problem the design is actually supposed to solve.

And what comes after — how it performs at small sizes, on packaging, next to other brand elements, six months from now when the context has changed.

A beautiful execution built on an empty brief is just decoration. It may look right. It rarely works right.

Before you hire a designer, ask for the brief they're designing from. If there isn't one, that's where the conversation needs to start.

---

**Post 2 — Invisible Research**

Your competitor's logo has no place in our conversation.

When I map the competitive landscape, clients don't see the research. No logos, no visual references, no stimuli that trigger reaction before analysis gets a chance.

But they do see the reasoning.

"Authentic local is overcrowded — anyone can claim it and no one owns it. Premium is available but you can't support it operationally yet. Here's what that leaves."

The logic is theirs to keep. The raw material stays mine.

Because a positioning you can't defend in a conversation isn't yours — it's borrowed. And borrowed positioning collapses the moment someone asks why.

## Comment Strategy

See the dedicated **Comment Framework** (`02-comment-framework.md`) for the complete strategy: evaluation criteria, voice of The Architect, comment architecture, post type patterns with examples, tracking signals, and active thread management.

### Summary for Quick Reference

**Why we comment:** Comments on others' posts build presence where the audience already exists. Potential clients don't post about brand strategy — they read what strategists and designers post. A strong comment next to a strategist or designer with authority implicitly positions you at the same level.

**Key principle — The Architect, not The Observer:**
- The Observer (Personal) reflects, doesn't name. The Architect (Goodspell) names the pattern precisely — so the reader sees the connection themselves.
- No educating, no correcting, no introducing Goodspell.
- Comment only when the post has **strategic intent** — the decision shown is derived from an insight, not from taste. Do not comment on pure execution without strategy upstream.

**Where not to comment:**
- High-volume, low-quality design market segment
- Posts with 12+ hashtags, CTA in post, sponsored content
- Design posts without strategic foundation — commenting there positions you as educator

**Frequency:** 2-3 strong comments per week. Consistency over volume.

**Track active threads** — author replies, conversation quality, next moves.

## Operating Rules

### Own Content (Posts)

- **Write in English** — target audience is international.
- **Structure:** Claim → Evidence → Conclusion. First line stops the scroll. Last line leaves tension open.
- **CTA — nuanced, not absent.** In Phase 1 (Recognition), no CTA at all. In Phase 2 (Contrast), subtle encouragement — "If this resonates, you know where to find me." In Phase 3 (Demonstrated Competence), direct but never urgent — "The next step is a short alignment call. No brief required. Bring the situation." Never create false urgency. Never ask the reader to act before they've been given reason to.
- **No hashtags.** Ever.
- **No explicit pitch.** The bio does the work.
- **Be specific** — reference real situations, not abstractions.
- **Consistency over volume** — 2-3 strong posts per week produce more credibility than daily posts of variable quality.

### Comments

See the complete **Comment Framework** (`02-comment-framework.md`). Key rules:
- 3-5 sentences maximum
- No CTA, no mention of Goodspell, no pitch
- Confirm direction → name the pattern → leave open
- Comment early — first hours after publication have the most active audience
- 2-3 comments per week, not more
- Do not comment on design posts without strategic intent

## The Positioning Cycle

The reader sees your comment once → ignores it.  
Sees it again on another post → remembers the name.  
A third time → visits your profile.  
At their right moment → writes the first message.

This cycle takes weeks or months. It is not shortened by volume. It is shortened by quality and consistency in the right places.
