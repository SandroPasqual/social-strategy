You wrote the proposal. You knew it was good.

The scope was right. The methodology was clear. The price was justified by everything in the document. You sent it with confidence.

Then came the questions.

Not about the work. About the number. Three exchanges that turned into five. A conversation that moved from evaluating the engagement to evaluating the price — and stayed there longer than it should have.

The proposal was solid. It needed a lawyer.

Not because the logic was weak — because the presence behind it hadn't established the authority the price required before the document arrived. The proposal did its job. It couldn't do the job the presence was supposed to do first.

When a buyer has already decided the price is right before they open the proposal, the questions are about scope. When they haven't, the questions are about the number.

The proposal isn't where that decision is made.
